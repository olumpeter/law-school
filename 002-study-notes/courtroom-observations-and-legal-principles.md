# Environment and Land Court (ELC) Study Notes

These notes summarize practical principles of Kenyan civil procedure as
encountered in Environment and Land Court (ELC) proceedings. They combine
statutory provisions with judicial decisions to explain not only what the law
is, but also the reasoning behind it.

---

# Preliminary Objection (P.O.)

## Definition

A **Preliminary Objection (P.O.)** is an objection raised at the outset of
proceedings that challenges the suit on a **pure point of law**.

It is determined solely on:

- the pleadings filed before the court; and
- the applicable law.

No evidence is required.

---

## Leading Authority

### Mukisa Biscuit Manufacturing Co. Ltd v West End Distributors Ltd [1969] EA 696

#### Principle

Law JA stated:

> "A Preliminary Objection consists of a pure point of law which has been
> pleaded, or which arises by clear implication out of pleadings, and which if
> argued as a preliminary point may dispose of the suit."

Sir Charles Newbold P further explained:

> "A Preliminary Objection is in the nature of what used to be a demurrer. It
> raises a pure point of law which is argued on the assumption that all the
> facts pleaded by the other side are correct. It cannot be raised if any fact
> has to be ascertained or if what is sought is the exercise of judicial
> discretion."

This remains the leading authority on Preliminary Objections in Kenya.

---

## Essential Characteristics

A proper Preliminary Objection:

- raises a pure point of law;
- assumes that all pleaded facts are true;
- does not require the court to investigate facts;
- does not require evidence; and
- may dispose of the entire suit or application if upheld.

Examples include objections based on:

- lack of jurisdiction;
- limitation of actions;
- res judicata;
- res sub judice;
- failure to comply with a mandatory statutory requirement.

---

## Evidence is Not Admissible

A Preliminary Objection must be determined without evidence.

The court should not consider:

- affidavits;
- witness statements;
- documentary exhibits;
- oral testimony; or
- disputed facts.

If evidence is necessary to determine the issue, then the objection is not a
proper Preliminary Objection.

---

## Practical Observation

A useful practical test is to ask:

> **Can the judge determine this issue without reading an affidavit or hearing a
> witness?**

If the answer is **No**, the matter should generally not be raised as a
Preliminary Objection.

Instead, it should ordinarily be brought before the court by way of a Notice of
Motion supported by evidence.

---

## Personal Understanding

- A Preliminary Objection challenges the legal validity of a suit or
  application.
- It is argued solely on the pleadings and the law.
- All pleaded facts are assumed to be true.
- No evidence is admissible.
- If evidence is required, the issue should ordinarily be raised through a
  Notice of Motion.

# Notice of Motion

## Definition

A **Notice of Motion** is a formal application made to the court requesting it
to exercise its judicial power by granting a specific order during the course of
proceedings.

Unlike a Preliminary Objection, a Notice of Motion may be supported by evidence.

It is one of the most common procedural applications in civil litigation.

---

## Statutory Foundation

A Notice of Motion is ordinarily brought under:

- **Order 51 Rule 1 of the Civil Procedure Rules**, which provides that every
  application to the court shall be by motion unless otherwise provided; and
- the specific statutory provisions under which the relief is sought.

---

## Purpose

A Notice of Motion enables a party to request the court to make an interlocutory
or procedural order before the final determination of the suit.

Examples include applications for:

- temporary injunctions;
- stay of execution;
- leave to amend pleadings;
- extension of time;
- review of a judgment or order;
- striking out pleadings;
- joinder or substitution of parties; and
- setting aside ex parte orders.

---

## Supporting Evidence

Unlike a Preliminary Objection, a Notice of Motion may be supported by evidence.

Such evidence commonly includes:

- a Supporting Affidavit;
- annexures (marked as exhibits);
- documentary evidence; and
- where permitted, additional affidavits.

The court considers both the applicable law and the evidence before determining
the application.

---

## Why Evidence is Permitted

A Notice of Motion often requires the court to determine disputed factual
issues.

For example:

- whether a party is in possession of land;
- whether construction is ongoing;
- whether substantial loss is likely to occur;
- whether there has been unreasonable delay; or
- whether an injunction should issue.

These are factual matters that cannot ordinarily be determined from the
pleadings alone.

Evidence is therefore necessary to enable the court to exercise its discretion
properly.

---

## Practical Observation

A useful practical test is to ask:

> **Does the court need evidence before it can decide the issue?**

If the answer is **Yes**, the matter should ordinarily be brought by way of a
Notice of Motion supported by a Supporting Affidavit.

If the answer is **No**, and the issue raises a pure point of law, it may
properly be raised as a Preliminary Objection.

---

## Preliminary Objection vs Notice of Motion

The fundamental distinction is that:

- a Preliminary Objection challenges the legal validity of proceedings without
  evidence; whereas
- a Notice of Motion requests the court to exercise its judicial discretion
  after considering both the law and the evidence.

---

## Personal Understanding

- A Notice of Motion is an application asking the court to make a specific
  order.
- It may be supported by affidavits, annexures and other documentary evidence.
- The court determines the application based on both the law and the evidence.
- Whenever the determination of an issue requires evidence, the appropriate
  procedure will ordinarily be a Notice of Motion rather than a Preliminary
  Objection.

# Why the Ruling Began with a Notice of Motion

## Practical Observation

Many rulings begin with words such as:

> "The Notice of Motion dated..."

This does **not** necessarily mean that the suit itself was commenced by a
Notice of Motion.

In most cases, the court is determining an **interlocutory application** that
has been filed within an already existing suit.

The ruling therefore begins with the application that is before the court, not
with the document that commenced the suit.

---

## Commencement of a Civil Suit

Under the Civil Procedure Act and the Civil Procedure Rules, a civil suit is
ordinarily commenced by:

- a Plaint;
- an Originating Summons;
- a Petition (where applicable);
- a Notice of Motion, but only where legislation specifically permits; or
- any other mode prescribed by law.

In ordinary land disputes before the Environment and Land Court, proceedings are
commonly commenced by a **Plaint**.

---

## Sequence of Proceedings

A typical Environment and Land Court matter may proceed as follows:

1. The Plaintiff files a **Plaint**, thereby commencing the suit.
2. A **Verifying Affidavit** accompanies the Plaint.
3. The Plaintiff files a **List of Witnesses**.
4. The Plaintiff files **Witness Statements**.
5. The Plaintiff files a **List of Documents** together with the documents
   intended to be relied upon at trial.
6. Before the suit is heard, a party files a **Notice of Motion** seeking
   interlocutory relief, such as:
   - a temporary injunction;
   - stay of execution;
   - leave to amend pleadings; or
   - any other interim order.
7. The court first determines the interlocutory application before proceeding to
   the hearing of the main suit.

---

## Why the Court Determines the Application First

Interlocutory applications are determined before the main suit because they
address matters requiring immediate judicial intervention.

For example, where land is about to be sold or a party faces imminent eviction,
waiting until the full trial may defeat the very purpose of the litigation.

The court therefore determines the interlocutory application first in order to
preserve the subject matter of the dispute pending the final hearing.

---

## Practical Observation

Whenever a ruling begins by referring to a Notice of Motion, ask yourself:

> **"Is the court determining the suit, or is it determining an application made
> within the suit?"**

In many instances, the answer is the latter.

---

## Personal Understanding

- A ruling that begins with a Notice of Motion does not necessarily mean that
  the suit was commenced by a Notice of Motion.
- The court is often determining an interlocutory application filed within an
  existing suit.
- The main suit usually continues after the interlocutory application has been
  decided.
- Interlocutory applications exist to preserve rights or the subject matter of
  the dispute pending the final determination of the case.

# Certificate of Urgency

## Definition

A **Certificate of Urgency** is a document filed together with an application
requesting the court to hear the application immediately, without waiting for
the ordinary scheduling process.

It does **not** determine the merits of the application.

Rather, it asks the court to determine **when** the application should be heard.

---

## Purpose

The purpose of a Certificate of Urgency is to persuade the court that delaying
the hearing of the application is likely to occasion injustice.

The applicant must demonstrate that immediate judicial intervention is necessary
to preserve the rights of the parties or the subject matter of the dispute.

---

## When It Is Commonly Used

A Certificate of Urgency is commonly filed where there is a risk of imminent
harm, for example:

- imminent sale of disputed land;
- threatened eviction;
- ongoing trespass;
- demolition of buildings;
- destruction of property;
- commencement or continuation of construction on disputed land;
- imminent execution of a decree; or
- any circumstance where waiting for the ordinary hearing process may render the
  application nugatory.

---

## The Judge's First Consideration

When an application is filed under a Certificate of Urgency, the judge does
**not** immediately determine the application itself.

The judge first considers whether the application is genuinely urgent.

The court may:

- certify the matter as urgent;
- decline to certify it as urgent; or
- issue interim directions pending an inter partes hearing.

Certification of urgency does **not** mean that the applicant has succeeded.

It simply means that the court is satisfied that the application deserves to be
heard sooner than it ordinarily would.

---

## Interim Orders

If the court is satisfied that immediate intervention is necessary, it may grant
temporary or interim orders pending the hearing of the application.

Examples include:

- temporary injunctions;
- temporary stay of execution;
- preservation orders; or
- status quo orders.

These orders are ordinarily intended to preserve the subject matter of the
dispute until all parties have been heard.

---

## Practical Observation

A Certificate of Urgency should never be confused with the substantive
application.

The Certificate answers the question:

> **"Why should this application be heard immediately?"**

The Notice of Motion answers a different question:

> **"What order is the applicant asking the court to grant?"**

Although they are often filed together, they serve different procedural
purposes.

---

## Personal Understanding

- A Certificate of Urgency requests the court to hear an application without
  delay.
- It does not determine the merits of the application.
- The judge first decides whether the matter is genuinely urgent.
- Certification of urgency does not mean that the application has succeeded.
- Its purpose is to ensure that justice is not defeated by delay.

# Trial Bundle

## Definition

A **Trial Bundle** is an organized collection of documents that a party intends
to rely upon during the hearing of a suit.

It enables the court and the parties to access documentary material in an
orderly and efficient manner during the trial.

A trial bundle is sometimes referred to as a **bundle of documents** or a
**document bundle**.

---

## Purpose

The purpose of a Trial Bundle is to:

- assist the judge in preparing for the hearing;
- enable advocates to refer to documents efficiently;
- enable witnesses to identify documents during testimony;
- facilitate cross-examination;
- maintain an orderly court record; and
- save judicial time during the hearing.

---

## Typical Contents

A Trial Bundle may contain:

- title documents;
- agreements or contracts;
- official searches;
- correspondence;
- maps or survey plans;
- photographs;
- valuation reports;
- receipts;
- demand letters;
- government records; and
- any other documents intended to be relied upon during the trial.

---

## Important Principle

A document included in a Trial Bundle is **not automatically evidence**.

Its inclusion in the bundle merely indicates that a party intends to rely upon
it during the hearing.

The document must still be formally produced before the court.

---

## When Does a Document Become Evidence?

A document ordinarily becomes evidence only after:

1. it is identified by a competent witness;
2. it is produced as an exhibit; and
3. the court admits it into evidence.

Until then, it remains merely a document contained in the Trial Bundle.

---

## Exhibit vs Trial Bundle

It is important to distinguish between a **document in the Trial Bundle** and an
**exhibit**.

A document in the Trial Bundle:

- has been filed with the court; but
- has not yet been admitted into evidence.

An exhibit:

- has been produced through a witness; and
- has been admitted by the court as evidence.

Only exhibits form part of the evidentiary record upon which the court
determines the facts in dispute.

---

## Practical Observation

Many first-year law students assume that filing documents automatically makes
them evidence.

This is incorrect.

The proper sequence is:

1. File the document.
2. Include it in the Trial Bundle.
3. Call a witness.
4. The witness identifies the document.
5. The document is produced.
6. The court admits it as an exhibit.

Only then does it become evidence.

---

## Personal Understanding

- A Trial Bundle is an organizational tool, not evidence.
- Filing a document does not automatically make it evidence.
- A document becomes evidence only after it is properly produced through a
  witness and admitted by the court as an exhibit.
- The Trial Bundle assists the court and the parties in conducting an orderly
  and efficient trial.

# De Novo

## Definition

**De novo** is a Latin expression meaning:

> **"afresh"**, **"anew"**, or **"from the beginning."**

When a court orders that a matter shall proceed **de novo**, it directs that the
case be heard again as though the earlier proceedings had not taken place.

The previous hearing is disregarded for purposes of determining the dispute.

---

## Purpose

A de novo hearing ensures that justice is done where the previous proceedings
cannot fairly be relied upon.

The objective is to give the parties a fresh and impartial hearing.

---

## When a Matter May Proceed De Novo

A court may order a de novo hearing in circumstances such as:

- the trial judge has retired, died, or been transferred before completing the
  case;
- the previous proceedings are declared a nullity;
- there has been a serious procedural irregularity;
- a higher court orders a retrial; or
- the interests of justice require the matter to be heard afresh.

---

## Effect of a De Novo Hearing

Where a matter proceeds de novo:

- the case starts afresh;
- witnesses may be recalled to testify again;
- documents are produced again during the new hearing;
- the court reaches its own findings based solely on the evidence presented
  during the fresh hearing.

The new court is not bound by factual findings made during the earlier
proceedings.

---

## De Novo vs Appeal

A de novo hearing should not be confused with an appeal.

### De Novo Hearing

The court hears the case again from the beginning.

The evidence is presented afresh.

The court determines both the facts and the law.

### Appeal

The appellate court ordinarily reviews the decision made by the lower court.

It considers whether the trial court correctly applied the law and properly
evaluated the evidence already on record.

Except where the law permits otherwise, an appeal is **not** a fresh trial.

---

## Practical Observation

Whenever you encounter the words:

> **"The matter shall proceed de novo."**

Ask yourself:

> **"Is the court reviewing the previous proceedings, or is it directing that
> the entire hearing begin again?"**

The answer is the latter.

The previous proceedings no longer form the basis upon which the dispute will be
determined.

---

## Personal Understanding

- **De novo** means "afresh" or "from the beginning."
- A de novo hearing treats the earlier proceedings as though they had not
  occurred.
- Witnesses and documents are ordinarily presented again during the fresh
  hearing.
- A de novo hearing is fundamentally different from an appeal because the court
  hears the case anew rather than reviewing an earlier decision.

# Key Distinctions

Understanding the distinction between procedural documents is essential in civil
litigation. Although these documents may appear similar, each serves a distinct
purpose within the judicial process.

---

# Preliminary Objection vs Notice of Motion

| Preliminary Objection                                                   | Notice of Motion                                                                              |
| ----------------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Raises a pure point of law.                                             | Requests the court to make a specific order.                                                  |
| Determined solely on the pleadings and the applicable law.              | Determined on both the law and the evidence.                                                  |
| No evidence is admissible.                                              | May be supported by affidavits, annexures and documentary evidence.                           |
| Assumes that all pleaded facts are true.                                | The court may determine disputed factual issues.                                              |
| Does not involve the exercise of judicial discretion based on evidence. | Frequently requires the court to exercise judicial discretion after considering the evidence. |
| May dispose of the entire suit if upheld.                               | Usually seeks interlocutory or procedural relief during the pendency of the suit.             |

---

## Practical Test

When deciding whether a matter should be raised by a Preliminary Objection or a
Notice of Motion, ask:

> **Can the court determine the issue without receiving evidence?**

- **Yes** → It may properly be raised as a Preliminary Objection.
- **No** → It should ordinarily be raised by a Notice of Motion supported by
  evidence.

---

# Plaint vs Notice of Motion

| Plaint                                           | Notice of Motion                                                                         |
| ------------------------------------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Commences a civil suit.                          | Makes an application within an existing suit.                                            |
| Sets out the Plaintiff's cause of action.        | Requests the court to grant a specific order.                                            |
| Defines the issues to be determined at trial.    | Seeks interim, interlocutory or procedural relief.                                       |
| Leads to the final determination of the dispute. | Does not ordinarily determine the substantive rights of the parties.                     |
| Filed once at the commencement of proceedings.   | May be filed at any stage of the proceedings whenever relief from the court is required. |

---

## Practical Observation

A useful way of distinguishing the two is to ask:

### Plaint

> **"Why should the court hear this dispute?"**

The Plaint answers this question by setting out the cause of action.

### Notice of Motion

> **"What order is the court being asked to make today?"**

The Notice of Motion answers this question by specifying the relief sought.

---

# Preliminary Objection vs Plaint

These documents serve entirely different functions.

A Preliminary Objection challenges the legal competency of a suit.

A Plaint commences the suit itself.

The court first receives the Plaint.

If a legal defect exists—for example, lack of jurisdiction or limitation of
actions—the Defendant may raise a Preliminary Objection challenging the suit
without requiring the court to hear evidence.

---

# Personal Understanding

- A Plaint starts the suit.
- A Notice of Motion asks the court to make an order within an existing suit.
- A Preliminary Objection challenges the legal validity of proceedings on a pure
  point of law.
- Whenever evidence is required, the appropriate procedure will ordinarily be a
  Notice of Motion rather than a Preliminary Objection.
- Understanding the purpose of each procedural document makes civil procedure
  much easier than attempting to memorise individual rules.

# Stay of Execution Pending Appeal

## Definition

A **stay of execution** is an order of the court temporarily suspending the
enforcement of a judgment or decree pending the determination of an appeal.

It preserves the status quo until the appellate court has an opportunity to
determine whether the decision appealed from should stand.

A stay of execution does **not** set aside or reverse the judgment.

It merely postpones its enforcement.

---

## Statutory Foundation

Applications for stay of execution before the High Court are principally
governed by:

- **Order 42 Rule 6 of the Civil Procedure Rules, 2010.**

The Rule recognizes that the mere filing of an appeal does not automatically
suspend the execution of a decree.

---

## General Principle

The filing of an appeal does **not** operate as a stay of execution.

The successful litigant remains entitled to execute the judgment unless the
court orders otherwise.

Order 42 Rule 6(1) provides:

> "No appeal or second appeal shall operate as a stay of execution or
> proceedings under a decree or order appealed from except in so far as the
> court appealed from may order..."

Thus, an appeal and a stay of execution are two distinct matters.

An appellant who wishes to prevent execution must make a separate application
for stay.

---

## Purpose of a Stay

The purpose of a stay of execution is not to deny the successful party the
fruits of the judgment.

Rather, it seeks to preserve the subject matter of the appeal so that, if the
appeal ultimately succeeds, the success is not rendered meaningless.

The court therefore balances two competing rights:

- the respondent's right to enjoy the fruits of a lawful judgment; and
- the appellant's right to pursue an effective appeal.

---

## Stay is a Discretionary Remedy

The grant of a stay of execution is **not automatic**.

It is a discretionary remedy.

The court exercises its discretion judicially after considering:

- the law;
- the facts of the case; and
- the interests of justice.

An applicant therefore has no automatic entitlement to a stay merely because an
appeal has been filed.

---

## Leading Authority

### Butt v Rent Restriction Tribunal [1979] KLR 417

#### Principle

The Court of Appeal held that the power to grant or refuse a stay of execution
is discretionary.

The discretion should be exercised in such a manner that, where appropriate, a
successful appeal is **not rendered nugatory**.

The court must therefore balance the competing interests of both parties while
ensuring that justice is done.

---

## Practical Observation

Many litigants mistakenly believe that filing a Notice of Appeal automatically
stops execution.

This is incorrect.

The correct sequence is:

1. Judgment is delivered.
2. An appeal is filed.
3. Execution may still proceed.
4. The appellant files a separate application for stay of execution.
5. The court determines whether a stay should be granted.

---

## Personal Understanding

- Filing an appeal does not automatically stop execution.
- A stay of execution is a separate remedy that must be specifically applied
  for.
- The purpose of a stay is to preserve the subject matter of the appeal.
- The court exercises judicial discretion when deciding whether to grant or
  refuse a stay.
- The court seeks to balance the appellant's right of appeal against the
  respondent's right to enjoy the fruits of the judgment.

# Conditions for Granting a Stay of Execution

## Statutory Foundation

The conditions for granting a stay of execution are contained in **Order 42 Rule
6(2) of the Civil Procedure Rules, 2010**.

The Rule provides that no order for stay of execution shall be made unless the
court is satisfied that:

1. substantial loss may result to the applicant unless the order is made;
2. the application has been made without unreasonable delay; and
3. the applicant gives such security as the court orders for the due performance
   of the decree or order.

These are statutory requirements that guide the court in the exercise of its
discretion.

---

## The Three Requirements

An applicant seeking a stay of execution must ordinarily satisfy **all three
requirements**.

Failure to satisfy any one of them may result in the application being
dismissed.

The court considers each requirement independently before deciding whether to
grant the stay.

---

## 1. Substantial Loss

The applicant must demonstrate that, unless execution is stayed, he or she is
likely to suffer **substantial loss**.

This is the most important requirement.

The applicant must show that execution will cause prejudice that cannot
adequately be remedied if the appeal ultimately succeeds.

---

## 2. No Unreasonable Delay

The application must be made promptly after the judgment or order complained of.

Delay may suggest that the applicant is not genuinely in need of the court's
protection.

The longer the delay, the greater the burden on the applicant to explain it.

---

## 3. Security for Due Performance

The applicant must be willing to provide security for the due performance of the
decree.

Security protects the successful litigant in the event that the appeal fails.

The nature and amount of the security are matters for the court's discretion.

Examples include:

- depositing money in court;
- providing a bank guarantee; or
- any other security that the court considers appropriate.

---

## Judicial Discretion

Even where the statutory conditions are satisfied, the court retains discretion.

The court must exercise that discretion judicially and fairly, taking into
account the circumstances of the particular case.

The objective is to strike a balance between:

- protecting the appellant from irreparable prejudice; and
- safeguarding the respondent's right to enjoy the fruits of a lawful judgment.

---

## Leading Authority

### Vishram Ravji Halai v Thornton & Turpin (1963) Ltd Civil Application No. Nai. 15 of 1990 [1990] KLR 365

#### Principle

The Court of Appeal held that the High Court's jurisdiction to grant a stay of
execution under Order 42 Rule 6 is subject to the statutory conditions set out
in the Rule.

Unlike the Court of Appeal, whose jurisdiction is derived from its own Rules,
the High Court must satisfy itself that the statutory requirements have been met
before granting a stay.

---

## Practical Observation

When reading an application for stay of execution, immediately ask three
questions:

1. Has the applicant demonstrated substantial loss?
2. Was the application made without unreasonable delay?
3. Has the applicant offered adequate security?

These questions mirror the requirements of Order 42 Rule 6(2) and provide a
useful checklist when analysing any application for stay.

---

## Personal Understanding

- Order 42 Rule 6(2) lays down three statutory conditions for granting a stay of
  execution.
- Substantial loss is the primary consideration.
- The application must be made promptly.
- Security protects the successful litigant while the appeal is pending.
- The court exercises judicial discretion only after considering these statutory
  requirements.

# Substantial Loss

## Definition

**Substantial loss** is the most important consideration in an application for
stay of execution.

It refers to real, serious or irreparable prejudice that the applicant is likely
to suffer if execution proceeds before the appeal is heard.

The applicant must demonstrate that, unless a stay is granted, the appeal will
be rendered ineffective or practically worthless.

---

## The Cornerstone of an Application for Stay

The courts have consistently held that substantial loss is the **cornerstone**
of an application for stay of execution.

Without proof of substantial loss, an application for stay will ordinarily fail,
even where the applicant has filed the application promptly and is willing to
provide security.

The burden rests upon the applicant to demonstrate the nature of the loss likely
to be suffered.

---

## Execution Alone is Not Substantial Loss

The mere fact that:

- execution has commenced;
- property has been attached;
- property has been proclaimed; or
- a decree is about to be executed,

does **not**, by itself, amount to substantial loss.

Execution is a lawful consequence of a valid judgment.

The applicant must therefore prove additional circumstances showing that
execution will occasion substantial prejudice.

---

## What Must the Applicant Prove?

The applicant should demonstrate that execution is likely to create a state of
affairs that cannot easily be reversed if the appeal succeeds.

Examples include:

- the respondent is unlikely to refund the decretal sum if the appeal succeeds;
- the subject matter of the dispute may be destroyed or permanently altered;
- unique property may be sold to third parties;
- the appeal may become practically worthless even if ultimately successful.

The court examines the particular facts of each case.

---

## Preservation of the Status Quo

One of the purposes of granting a stay is to preserve the **status quo** pending
the determination of the appeal.

Preserving the status quo ensures that the appellate court is able to determine
the appeal without the subject matter having been irreversibly altered.

---

## Leading Authorities

### James Wangalwa & Another v Agnes Naliaka Cheseto [2012] eKLR

#### Principle

The High Court held that:

> "The fact that the process of execution has been put in motion, or is likely
> to be put in motion, by itself does not amount to substantial loss."

The court further observed that execution is a lawful process.

The applicant must therefore establish other factors demonstrating that
execution will create a state of affairs that will irreparably affect or negate
the very essential core of the applicant as the successful party in the appeal.

---

### Silverstein v Chesoni [2002] 1 KLR 867

#### Principle

The High Court stated that:

> **Substantial loss is the cornerstone of both jurisdictions for granting a
> stay.**

Without proof of substantial loss, there is ordinarily no basis for the court to
exercise its discretion in favour of the applicant.

---

### Mukuma v Abuoga [1988] KLR 645

#### Principle

The Court of Appeal explained that substantial loss is what must be prevented by
preserving the status quo because such loss would render the appeal nugatory.

The purpose of a stay is therefore to ensure that, if the appeal succeeds, the
success is meaningful in practice and not merely theoretical.

---

## Practical Observation

Whenever reading an application for stay of execution, ask:

> **What specific loss will occur if execution proceeds today?**

If the only answer is:

> "Execution will take place."

that is **not enough**.

The applicant must identify the **additional prejudice** that will arise from
execution and explain why that prejudice cannot adequately be remedied if the
appeal eventually succeeds.

---

## Personal Understanding

- Substantial loss is the most important requirement for obtaining a stay of
  execution.
- Execution itself is lawful and does not automatically amount to substantial
  loss.
- The applicant must prove additional facts showing that execution will cause
  serious or irreparable prejudice.
- A stay preserves the status quo so that the appeal, if successful, is not
  rendered nugatory.
- The courts repeatedly describe substantial loss as the **cornerstone** of an
  application for stay of execution.

# Nugatory Appeal

## Definition

The word **nugatory** means:

- worthless;
- useless;
- futile; or
- incapable of producing any practical benefit.

An appeal is said to be **rendered nugatory** when, although the appellate court
may eventually allow the appeal, the success is of no practical value because
irreversible events have already occurred.

---

## Why the Court Grants a Stay

One of the primary purposes of granting a stay of execution is to ensure that a
successful appeal is **not rendered nugatory**.

The court therefore asks:

> **If execution proceeds today and the appeal succeeds tomorrow, will the
> success of the appeal have any practical value?**

If the answer is **No**, the court is more likely to grant a stay, provided the
statutory requirements have been satisfied.

---

## Practical Examples

### Example 1: Sale of Land

A parcel of land is sold through execution to an innocent purchaser before the
appeal is heard.

Two years later, the Court of Appeal allows the appeal.

Although the appellant has succeeded, recovery of the land may no longer be
possible.

The appeal has therefore been rendered **nugatory**.

---

### Example 2: Payment of Money

A judgment debtor pays a substantial decretal sum during execution.

The respondent has no known assets and is unlikely to refund the money if the
appeal succeeds.

If the appeal is later allowed, the appellant's victory may be meaningless
because recovery of the money is impossible.

Again, the appeal has been rendered **nugatory**.

---

## The Court's Balancing Exercise

Whenever considering an application for stay, the court balances two competing
interests.

### The Respondent

The respondent asks:

> "I have obtained a lawful judgment. Why should I be prevented from enjoying
> the fruits of my judgment?"

### The Appellant

The appellant asks:

> "If execution proceeds before my appeal is heard, my appeal will become
> useless."

The court seeks to balance these competing rights fairly.

A stay should neither unjustly delay the respondent nor render the appellant's
appeal worthless.

---

## Leading Authority

### Butt v Rent Restriction Tribunal [1979] KLR 417

#### Principle

The Court of Appeal held that judicial discretion should be exercised in such a
manner that a successful appeal is **not rendered nugatory**.

This principle remains one of the guiding considerations whenever a court
determines an application for stay of execution.

---

## Relationship with Substantial Loss

The concepts of **substantial loss** and a **nugatory appeal** are closely
related.

The applicant proves substantial loss by demonstrating that execution is likely
to create irreversible consequences which would deprive the appeal of any
practical value.

In many cases, substantial loss exists precisely because the appeal is likely to
be rendered nugatory if execution proceeds.

---

## Practical Observation

Whenever you encounter the expression:

> **"The appeal will be rendered nugatory."**

Ask yourself:

> **"If the appellant eventually wins, will that victory still have practical
> value?"**

If the answer is **No**, you have understood why the applicant is seeking a stay
of execution.

---

## Personal Understanding

- A nugatory appeal is an appeal whose success would have no practical benefit.
- One purpose of a stay of execution is to prevent an appeal from being rendered
  nugatory.
- The court balances the respondent's right to enjoy the fruits of the judgment
  against the appellant's right to pursue an effective appeal.
- The concepts of substantial loss and a nugatory appeal are closely connected
  because both seek to prevent irreversible injustice pending the determination
  of the appeal.

# Security for Due Performance of the Decree

## Definition

**Security for the due performance of the decree** is a guarantee provided by
the applicant to ensure that, if the appeal ultimately fails, the respondent
will still be able to enjoy the fruits of the judgment.

It is one of the mandatory statutory requirements under **Order 42 Rule 6(2)(b)
of the Civil Procedure Rules**.

---

## Purpose

A stay of execution delays the successful party from enforcing a lawful
judgment.

The law therefore requires the applicant to provide security so that the
respondent is not unfairly prejudiced by that delay.

Security strikes a balance between the competing rights of both parties.

It protects:

- the appellant from irreversible harm pending the appeal; and
- the respondent against the risk that the decree may never be satisfied if the
  appeal fails.

---

## Why Security is Required

Suppose the court grants a stay of execution.

The respondent must wait until the appeal is determined before enforcing the
decree.

If the appeal ultimately fails, the respondent should not be left wondering
whether the appellant is still capable of satisfying the judgment.

Security provides that assurance.

---

## Nature of Security

The Civil Procedure Rules do not prescribe a single form of security.

The court has discretion to determine what constitutes appropriate security
depending on the circumstances of each case.

Examples include:

- depositing the decretal sum in court;
- depositing part of the decretal sum in court;
- providing a bank guarantee;
- depositing money in a joint interest-earning account held by the advocates for
  both parties; or
- any other security that the court considers sufficient.

---

## Judicial Discretion

The amount and form of security are matters for the court's discretion.

The court seeks to impose security that is:

- reasonable;
- proportionate; and
- sufficient to protect the respondent without imposing unnecessary hardship
  upon the appellant.

The court therefore considers the circumstances of each case rather than
applying a fixed rule.

---

## Security is Not a Punishment

Security should not be viewed as a penalty imposed upon the appellant.

Neither is it intended to discourage appeals.

Its purpose is simply to protect the respondent while the execution of the
decree is temporarily suspended.

---

## Relationship with the Right of Appeal

The requirement for security reflects an important principle of civil procedure.

The appellant has a constitutional and statutory right to appeal.

The respondent has an equally important right to enjoy the fruits of a lawful
judgment.

Security enables both rights to coexist fairly.

---

## Practical Observation

Whenever reading an application for stay of execution, ask:

> **"How does the applicant propose to protect the respondent if the appeal
> fails?"**

The answer to that question is the applicant's proposed security.

---

## Personal Understanding

- Security is one of the mandatory requirements for obtaining a stay of
  execution.
- It protects the successful litigant while execution is temporarily suspended.
- The court has discretion to determine the form and amount of security.
- Security is not a punishment; it is a safeguard that balances the rights of
  both parties.
- A stay of execution should not leave the respondent in a worse position if the
  appeal ultimately fails.

# Delay in Applying for a Stay of Execution

## Statutory Foundation

Under **Order 42 Rule 6(2)(a) of the Civil Procedure Rules, 2010**, an applicant
seeking a stay of execution must demonstrate that:

> **"the application has been made without unreasonable delay."**

Promptness is therefore one of the statutory conditions for obtaining a stay.

---

## Purpose

The law requires an applicant to act promptly because a stay of execution is an
equitable and discretionary remedy.

A party who genuinely fears substantial loss should seek the court's protection
without unnecessary delay.

Unexplained delay may indicate that the application is merely intended to
obstruct or delay the successful party from enjoying the fruits of the judgment.

---

## What Amounts to Unreasonable Delay?

The Civil Procedure Rules do not prescribe a fixed number of days or months that
constitute unreasonable delay.

Whether delay is unreasonable depends upon:

- the length of the delay;
- the explanation offered by the applicant;
- the circumstances of the particular case; and
- whether the delay has prejudiced the respondent.

Each case is therefore determined on its own facts.

---

## Delay Must Be Explained

Delay does not automatically defeat an application for stay.

However, where there has been a significant lapse of time, the applicant bears
the burden of explaining the delay.

Examples of possible explanations include:

- delay in obtaining certified copies of the judgment or decree;
- illness;
- circumstances beyond the applicant's control; or
- any other reasonable explanation accepted by the court.

If no satisfactory explanation is offered, the court may refuse to exercise its
discretion in favour of the applicant.

---

## Why Promptness Matters

The successful litigant is entitled to enjoy the fruits of a lawful judgment
without unnecessary delay.

If applicants were permitted to seek stays months or years after judgment
without explanation, the administration of justice would be undermined.

The requirement of promptness therefore promotes fairness to both parties.

---

## Relationship with Judicial Discretion

Prompt filing does not automatically entitle an applicant to a stay.

Likewise, delay does not automatically defeat the application.

The court considers delay together with:

- substantial loss;
- security for the due performance of the decree; and
- all the circumstances of the case.

The ultimate question remains whether it is just to grant the stay.

---

## Practical Observation

Whenever analysing an application for stay of execution, ask:

> **"How long did the applicant wait before filing the application?"**

Then ask:

> **"Has that delay been satisfactorily explained?"**

The court will ordinarily consider both questions before exercising its
discretion.

---

## Personal Understanding

- An application for stay must be made without unreasonable delay.
- What amounts to unreasonable delay depends on the circumstances of each case.
- Where delay exists, the applicant should explain it satisfactorily.
- Promptness demonstrates a genuine need for the court's protection.
- Delay is one factor the court considers alongside substantial loss and
  security when deciding whether to grant a stay.

# Judicial Discretion in Granting a Stay of Execution

## Definition

A **judicial discretion** is the lawful authority given to a court to choose
between two or more legally permissible courses of action based on the facts and
circumstances of a particular case.

Judicial discretion is **not** the exercise of personal preference or opinion.

It must be exercised:

- judicially;
- reasonably;
- fairly; and
- in accordance with established legal principles.

---

## Discretion under Order 42 Rule 6

The power to grant or refuse a stay of execution is discretionary.

Even where an applicant satisfies the statutory requirements under Order 42 Rule
6(2), the court must still determine whether, in all the circumstances of the
case, it is just to grant the stay.

The court therefore considers both:

- the statutory requirements; and
- the interests of justice.

---

## The Purpose of Judicial Discretion

Judicial discretion enables the court to achieve fairness between the parties.

The court seeks to balance two competing rights:

### The Appellant's Right

The appellant is entitled to pursue an appeal without the appeal being rendered
nugatory.

### The Respondent's Right

The respondent is entitled to enjoy the fruits of a lawful judgment without
unnecessary delay.

Neither right is absolute.

The court must therefore strike a fair balance between them.

---

## Judicial Discretion is Guided by Principle

Judicial discretion is never exercised arbitrarily.

It is guided by:

- the Civil Procedure Rules;
- applicable statutes;
- judicial precedents; and
- the particular facts of the case.

A judge must therefore give legal reasons for granting or refusing a stay.

---

## Leading Authority

### Butt v Rent Restriction Tribunal [1979] KLR 417

#### Principle

The Court of Appeal held that:

- the power to grant or refuse a stay of execution is discretionary;
- the discretion should be exercised in a manner that does not render a
  successful appeal nugatory; and
- each application should be determined according to its own particular
  circumstances.

This remains one of the leading authorities governing applications for stay of
execution.

---

## Judicial Discretion vs Judicial Arbitrariness

It is important to distinguish between **discretion** and **arbitrariness**.

### Judicial Discretion

A judge:

- applies the law;
- considers the evidence;
- weighs the competing interests; and
- gives reasons for the decision.

### Judicial Arbitrariness

A judge would act arbitrarily if the decision were based upon:

- personal opinion;
- emotion;
- favouritism;
- prejudice; or
- irrelevant considerations.

Such a decision would be contrary to the rule of law.

---

## Practical Observation

Whenever a judgment states:

> **"The grant of a stay is discretionary."**

Do not interpret this to mean that the judge may decide the application however
he or she wishes.

Instead, understand that the court must exercise its discretion according to
established legal principles and provide reasons for its decision.

---

## Personal Understanding

- A stay of execution is a discretionary remedy.
- Judicial discretion is the lawful exercise of judgment based upon legal
  principles and the facts of the case.
- The court balances the appellant's right of appeal against the respondent's
  right to enjoy the fruits of the judgment.
- Judicial discretion must always be exercised fairly, reasonably and in
  accordance with the law.
- A judge must provide legal reasons for granting or refusing a stay of
  execution.

# Status Quo

## Definition

The **status quo** refers to the existing state of affairs at a particular point
in time.

When a court orders that the **status quo be maintained**, it directs the
parties to preserve the existing situation until the court determines the
dispute or until further orders are made.

The objective is to prevent either party from altering the subject matter of the
litigation before the court has an opportunity to determine the rights of the
parties.

---

## Purpose

An order maintaining the status quo is intended to:

- preserve the subject matter of the dispute;
- prevent irreversible changes;
- ensure that the court's final decision remains effective; and
- prevent either party from gaining an unfair advantage during the pendency of
  the proceedings.

---

## Relationship with Stay of Execution

Although a **stay of execution** and a **status quo order** both preserve the
existing position, they are not the same.

A stay of execution:

- suspends the enforcement of a decree or order.

A status quo order:

- preserves the existing factual situation between the parties.

The two remedies may sometimes achieve similar practical results, but they serve
different legal purposes.

---

## Examples

### Example 1: Land Dispute

The Plaintiff is in possession of the disputed land.

The Defendant threatens to evict the Plaintiff before the suit is heard.

The court may order that:

> **"The status quo obtaining on today's date shall be maintained."**

Neither party may change the existing position until further orders of the
court.

---

### Example 2: Construction on Disputed Land

A party begins constructing permanent buildings on land whose ownership is
disputed.

The court may order that the status quo be maintained.

This prevents further construction until the dispute has been determined.

---

### Example 3: Stay of Execution

A judgment orders the sale of land through execution.

The judgment debtor appeals and applies for a stay.

If the court grants a stay, the execution process is suspended.

The practical effect is that the existing position is preserved while the appeal
is pending.

---

## Relationship with Substantial Loss

Preserving the status quo is often necessary to prevent **substantial loss**.

If the subject matter is destroyed, transferred, demolished or substantially
altered before the appeal is heard, the appeal may ultimately be rendered
**nugatory**.

Maintaining the status quo therefore protects the effectiveness of the appellate
process.

---

## Leading Authority

### Mukuma v Abuoga [1988] KLR 645

#### Principle

The Court of Appeal observed that the purpose of granting a stay is to preserve
the status quo so that substantial loss is prevented and a successful appeal is
not rendered nugatory.

The court seeks to ensure that the appeal remains capable of producing a
practical and meaningful result.

---

## Practical Observation

Whenever you read that the court has ordered:

> **"Status quo shall be maintained."**

Ask yourself:

> **"What existing state of affairs is the court trying to preserve?"**

The answer will usually identify the subject matter that the court considers
necessary to protect pending the determination of the dispute.

---

## Personal Understanding

- The status quo is the existing state of affairs.
- A status quo order preserves the subject matter of the dispute.
- It prevents irreversible changes before the court determines the parties'
  rights.
- Preserving the status quo helps prevent substantial loss and ensures that an
  appeal is not rendered nugatory.
- Although related, a status quo order is different from a stay of execution
  because each serves a distinct procedural purpose.

# Leading Authorities on Stay of Execution

The law governing stay of execution under **Order 42 Rule 6 of the Civil
Procedure Rules** has been developed through a series of judicial decisions.

Each authority contributes a distinct legal principle.

Understanding **what each case adds** is more valuable than merely memorizing
the case names.

---

## 1. Butt v Rent Restriction Tribunal [1979] KLR 417

### Principle

The Court of Appeal held that:

- the power to grant or refuse a stay of execution is discretionary;
- the discretion should be exercised judicially;
- the court should exercise its discretion in a manner that does not render a
  successful appeal nugatory; and
- each case must be determined according to its own circumstances.

### Contribution to the Law

This case explains **how judicial discretion should be exercised** when
determining an application for stay.

---

## 2. Vishram Ravji Halai v Thornton & Turpin (1963) Ltd Civil Application No. Nai. 15 of 1990 [1990] KLR 365

### Principle

The Court of Appeal held that the High Court's jurisdiction to grant a stay
under Order 42 Rule 6 is governed by the statutory conditions contained in that
Rule.

The High Court must therefore consider:

- substantial loss;
- unreasonable delay; and
- security for the due performance of the decree.

### Contribution to the Law

This case explains **the statutory framework** governing applications for stay
before the High Court.

---

## 3. James Wangalwa & Another v Agnes Naliaka Cheseto [2012] eKLR

### Principle

The High Court held that:

> The mere commencement of execution does not amount to substantial loss.

Execution is a lawful process.

The applicant must demonstrate additional circumstances showing that execution
is likely to occasion substantial prejudice.

### Contribution to the Law

This case explains **what substantial loss is not**.

It emphasizes that execution alone is insufficient to justify the grant of a
stay.

---

## 4. Silverstein v Chesoni [2002] 1 KLR 867

### Principle

The High Court stated that:

> **Substantial loss is the cornerstone of the jurisdiction to grant a stay of
> execution.**

Without proof of substantial loss, an application for stay will ordinarily fail.

### Contribution to the Law

This case identifies **the central importance of substantial loss** in every
application for stay.

---

## 5. Mukuma v Abuoga [1988] KLR 645

### Principle

The Court of Appeal explained that a stay of execution is intended to preserve
the status quo so that substantial loss is prevented and the appeal is not
rendered nugatory.

### Contribution to the Law

This case explains **why** courts grant stays of execution.

The purpose is to preserve the effectiveness of the appeal.

---

# How the Authorities Fit Together

Each authority answers a different legal question.

| Authority                                   | Legal Question Answered                                      |
| ------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Butt v Rent Restriction Tribunal**        | How should judicial discretion be exercised?                 |
| **Vishram Ravji Halai v Thornton & Turpin** | What statutory requirements govern a stay?                   |
| **James Wangalwa v Agnes Cheseto**          | Does execution alone amount to substantial loss?             |
| **Silverstein v Chesoni**                   | What is the most important requirement for a stay?           |
| **Mukuma v Abuoga**                         | Why should the court preserve the status quo pending appeal? |

---

## Practical Observation

When reading a judgment on stay of execution, do not treat the authorities as
repetitive.

Instead, ask:

> **"What new legal principle is this authority contributing?"**

Experienced advocates read authorities this way.

Rather than memorizing every paragraph, they identify the unique legal
proposition for which each case is cited.

---

## Personal Understanding

- Every leading authority contributes a different legal principle.
- **Butt** explains judicial discretion.
- **Vishram Ravji Halai** explains the statutory requirements under Order 42
  Rule 6.
- **James Wangalwa** explains that execution alone is not substantial loss.
- **Silverstein** identifies substantial loss as the cornerstone of a stay.
- **Mukuma** explains that a stay preserves the status quo so that an appeal is
  not rendered nugatory.
- Understanding the contribution of each authority makes it much easier to
  analyse future judgments on stay of execution.

# Practical Framework for Analysing an Application for Stay of Execution

Reading an application for stay of execution becomes much easier if the analysis
follows a logical sequence.

Instead of attempting to memorize Order 42 Rule 6, ask a series of practical
questions.

This is the same approach adopted by judges when writing rulings.

---

## Step 1: Has an Appeal Been Filed?

The court first determines whether there is:

- an appeal already filed; or
- an intention to appeal that is recognized by law.

A stay of execution is ancillary to an appeal.

Without an appeal, there is ordinarily no basis upon which to suspend execution.

---

## Step 2: Is Execution Capable of Proceeding?

The court considers whether there is a decree or order capable of being
executed.

Execution is the lawful enforcement of a judgment.

If there is no executable decree or order, an application for stay may be
unnecessary.

---

## Step 3: Has the Applicant Demonstrated Substantial Loss?

This is usually the first substantive issue considered.

The court asks:

> **"What specific prejudice will the applicant suffer if execution proceeds?"**

The applicant must demonstrate more than the mere fact that execution has
commenced.

The loss must be substantial.

---

## Step 4: Was the Application Made Without Unreasonable Delay?

The court considers whether the applicant acted promptly after the judgment or
order.

If there has been delay, the court examines whether a satisfactory explanation
has been provided.

---

## Step 5: Has Security Been Offered?

The court considers whether the applicant has proposed adequate security for the
due performance of the decree.

Security protects the respondent should the appeal ultimately fail.

---

## Step 6: Will Refusing the Stay Render the Appeal Nugatory?

Finally, the court asks whether execution is likely to defeat the practical
value of the appeal.

If execution will create irreversible consequences before the appeal is heard,
the court may consider granting a stay in order to preserve the effectiveness of
the appellate process.

---

# Judicial Reasoning

The reasoning process may be summarized as follows:

1. Is there an appeal?
2. Is there an executable decree?
3. Will the applicant suffer substantial loss?
4. Was the application filed without unreasonable delay?
5. Has adequate security been offered?
6. Will refusing a stay render the appeal nugatory?
7. If all relevant considerations favour the applicant, should the court
   exercise its discretion to grant a stay?

This sequence reflects the analytical approach commonly adopted by the courts.

---

## Practical Observation

Whenever reading a ruling on stay of execution, identify the paragraph where the
judge answers each of these questions.

Doing so transforms a lengthy judgment into a logical sequence of legal issues
rather than a collection of quotations and authorities.

---

## Personal Understanding

- Applications for stay should be analysed systematically rather than by
  memorising legal principles.
- The court follows a logical sequence of questions before exercising its
  discretion.
- Substantial loss remains the central consideration throughout the analysis.
- Every other requirement supports the ultimate objective of ensuring justice
  between the parties.
- Understanding the judge's reasoning process is more valuable than memorising
  individual cases or statutory provisions.
