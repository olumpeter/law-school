# Becoming a Lawyer

## A Personal Philosophy of Legal Education

Every profession requires knowledge.

Yet the legal profession requires something more. It requires the ability
to exercise judgment when the answer is uncertain, to reason carefully
when opinions differ, and to remain faithful to principle when the
easier path is compromise.

Knowledge enables a lawyer to understand the law. Judgment enables a
lawyer to apply it wisely. Character determines how that judgment is
ultimately exercised. The legal profession therefore demands not only an
educated mind but also a disciplined intellect and an honourable
character.

This manuscript is founded upon a simple conviction: becoming a lawyer
is not an event but a lifelong process of formation.

Admission to law school marks its beginning. Graduation celebrates an
important milestone. Admission to practice opens the door to a
profession. None of these moments, however significant, completes the
journey. They merely mark successive stages in the continuing formation
of a lawyer.

The law itself refuses to stand still. Legislatures enact new statutes.
Courts reconsider established principles. Society presents questions
that previous generations could not have imagined. Every generation of
lawyers therefore inherits both an ancient tradition and the
responsibility to apply it faithfully to new circumstances. For this
reason, the lawyer must remain a student throughout life.

This book was written before the first lecture, before the first
examination, and before the first legal argument. It was born from a
desire to understand not merely what lawyers know, but how they think,
how they learn, and what kind of people they must become.

Its purpose is not to replace textbooks, lectures, statutes, or judicial
decisions. Those remain the primary sources from which legal knowledge
is acquired. Rather, this manuscript offers a philosophy by which every
subject may be studied, every case examined, every statute interpreted,
and every lesson woven into the gradual formation of a legal mind.

Accordingly, these pages concern themselves less with legal doctrine
than with legal development. They do not promise shortcuts, guarantee
academic success, or offer techniques for passing examinations. Instead,
they advocate disciplined study, careful reading, patient reflection,
intellectual honesty, and continual revision. They proceed from the
belief that understanding is more valuable than memorization, that
reasoning is more valuable than recollection, and that character gives
lasting meaning to both.

If this manuscript accomplishes its purpose, the reader will finish it
with more than additional knowledge. The reader will possess a clearer
understanding of what it means to undertake the lifelong work of
becoming a lawyer.

## The Visible Curriculum and the Invisible Curriculum

Every law student is familiar with the visible curriculum.

It appears in university prospectuses, lecture timetables, course
outlines, and examination papers. It consists of the subjects that every
student is expected to study: Contract Law, Tort Law, Criminal Law,
Constitutional Law, Legal Research, Jurisprudence, and many others.
These subjects form the intellectual foundation of legal education, and
no student can become a competent lawyer without mastering them.

Yet legal education contains another curriculum.

It does not appear in the prospectus.

No lecturer introduces it during orientation.

No examination is devoted exclusively to it.

Many students complete law school without ever realizing that it exists.

Nevertheless, it is this second curriculum that quietly shapes the
quality of the lawyer they eventually become.

This is the invisible curriculum.

The invisible curriculum is not concerned primarily with what the
student knows. It is concerned with the kind of mind the student is
gradually developing.

It teaches patience when difficult questions resist immediate answers.

It teaches precision when careless language obscures meaning.

It teaches disciplined reasoning when competing arguments appear equally
persuasive.

It teaches intellectual humility when better evidence requires a change
of mind.

It teaches respect for authority without discouraging independent
thought.

Above all, it teaches judgment.

Unlike the visible curriculum, these lessons are rarely taught through
lectures. They are acquired slowly through the daily discipline of legal
study. They emerge while reading difficult cases, interpreting statutes,
constructing arguments, revising written work, questioning assumptions,
and discovering that careful reasoning often demands more effort than
quick opinion.

A student may excel in every examination and yet neglect this curriculum
entirely. Such a student may graduate possessing considerable legal
knowledge without ever developing mature legal judgment. Another student
may find the visible curriculum demanding, yet faithfully cultivate the
habits of careful thought, intellectual honesty, disciplined inquiry,
and continual improvement. In the long run, it is often this second
education that distinguishes the thoughtful lawyer from the merely
well-informed one.

This manuscript is devoted principally to that invisible curriculum.

It explores the habits, disciplines, and ways of thinking that shape a
lawyer long before that lawyer is entrusted with shaping the lives of
others through the law.

For knowledge may qualify a person to enter the legal profession.

Only judgment, integrity, and disciplined thought enable that person to
honour its responsibilities.
